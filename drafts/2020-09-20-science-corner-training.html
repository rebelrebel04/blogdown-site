    • Impact of exercise on vascular health
      • You know this already right? Exercise is good for your circulatory system. That’s why they call it “cardio”. But it’s actually more interesting than that — and in a way that underscores the importance of movement and resistance training above and beyond pure cardio.
      • Proposed mechanism: shear stress on vascular system (due to actual physical act of placing blood vessels under mechanical stress, i.e., movement and resistance training, not just “getting the blood flowing”) up-regulates markers of vascular health, in particular the vasodilator NO
      • So improving cardiovascular health is not just about “getting the blood flowing” but actually moving your blood vessels (which are, of course, part of the muscles that are being moved).
      • In fact, there is evidence that even static stretching has positive effects on vascular health for the same reason (stretching the blood vessels)

    • Cramping
      • No solid evidence for traditional theories (dehydration, electrolytes)
      • One theory is that cramping is due to fatigued muscle fibers, which are susceptible to abnormal increases in activity of muscle fibers and decreased activity in GTOs (Aetiology of skeletal muscle ‘cramps’ during exercise: a novel hypothesis. Schwellnus MP https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/?term=Schwellnus%20MP%5BAuthor%5D&cauthor=true&cauthor_uid=92325531, Derman EW https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/?term=Derman%20EW%5BAuthor%5D&cauthor=true&cauthor_uid=9232553, Noakes TD https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/?term=Noakes%20TD%5BAuthor%5D&cauthor=true&cauthor_uid=9232553.)
        • The inverse stretch reflex corroborates this theory: stretching the muscle alleviates cramping by activating the GTOs, which inhibit muscle fiber activity
